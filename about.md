        <h2>The Architecture of Belief</h2>
        <p>
          My work is an exploration of the intersection between complex systems—whether they are digital architectures or the fragile frameworks of human meaning. I started in business management, questioning the architectures of society and the mechanisms of decision-making, before finding in software engineering a way to construct meaning in the digital age.
        </p>
        <p>
          This foundation gives me a perspective that bridges the gap between technical execution and the <em>why</em> of product development. I am not just interested in how to build; I am interested in what we are building, and what these systems reveal about the people who inhabit them.
        </p>

        <h2>Engineering as Participation</h2>
        <p>
          Joining Semicolon Africa as a Software Engineering Intern was the moment this intellectual pursuit became practical. I learned that engineering is not merely code; it is a discipline of participation. Shipping production features, building APIs, and working in Agile teams is an exercise in managing complexity and creating order out of chaos—a theme that permeates everything I do, including my writing.
        </p>
        <p>
          It was intense. It was also exactly what I needed. I learned how to read and navigate a real codebase, how to write tests that actually protect the system, how to communicate clearly in engineering discussions, and how to ship with confidence. The gap between "learning to code" and "building software professionally" is wide — Semicolon Africa pushed me across it.
        </p>

        <h2>The Cybersecurity Chapter</h2>
        <p>
          As I grew as a software engineer, I became increasingly interested in the security layer. Every API I built raised a question: <em>how would someone attack this?</em> Every system I worked on made me wonder: <em>what are its weak points?</em>
        </p>
        <p>
          That curiosity turned into a structured learning roadmap. Today I'm actively studying Linux, networking fundamentals, ethical hacking, and security tools — with a clear target: professional competency in cybersecurity to complement my engineering work. In the digital economy, building and securing are two sides of the same coin.
        </p>

        <h2>Psychology, People & Product Thinking</h2>
        <p>
          One thing my business education gave me that I'll never trade is a genuine interest in human psychology. Why do people use the products they use? What motivates behavior? What makes something feel intuitive versus frustrating?
        </p>
        <p>
          This shapes how I approach product and engineering work. I care about the user sitting on the other side of the interface — not just the function call that powers it. That combination of technical skill and human empathy is what I mean when I say I build with a <em>human-centered</em> lens.
        </p>

        <h2>The Writing Life</h2>
        <p>
          In parallel with everything else, I am currently writing my first book. <em>The Lie We Were Taught</em> is a thought-provoking exploration of life, growth, spirituality, and what it truly means to be human. It's personal, it's philosophical, and it comes from years of reading, questioning, and reflecting.
        </p>
        <p>
          The <em>Learning To Be Human</em> series is just beginning, and this first installment is actively in progress.
        </p>

        <h2>What I'm Building Toward</h2>
        <p>
          My long-term goal is clear: build technology products that meaningfully improve how people connect, learn, and grow. That might look like a SaaS product, a startup, a developer tool, or something that doesn't have a name yet. What matters is the impact — the person whose life is measurably better because of something I built.
        </p>
        <p>
          I'm open to remote opportunities and international relocation. I want to work on hard problems with talented people, keep learning at a ridiculous pace, and earn the right to eventually build something entirely my own.
        </p>
        <p>
          That's the story. It's still being written.
        </p>
